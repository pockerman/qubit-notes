## Knowledge Distillation for LLMs

Large language models (LLMs) can deliver strong performance across a wide range of tasks. However, deploying them efficiently in production is another matter. 
Indeed model accuracy alone or any other equivalent performance metric, rarely determines which model will be deployed.
In fact there is a host of parameters one needs to consider (amongst other operational metrics):

- Inference latency
- Memory footprint
- Ease of scalability

Fine-tuned models like GPT-4 or LLaMA-2 65B may be accurate, but they’re often too large, expensive, or slow to use in real-world systems. That’s where knowledge distillation comes in.
Knowledge distillation allows you to transfer the behavior of a powerful, resource-heavy teacher model into a smaller, faster student model. 
The result of knowledge distillation is a lightweight model that performs like its larger counterpart, but runs faster and cheaper; this is of course ideal for production.

For example, a fine-tuned GPT-4 model might be great at answering customer support questions for an e-commerce company. 
But serving that model at scale may not be feasible. Instead, you can use GPT-4 to generate training data—high-quality answers to common questions—and then fine-tune a smaller model like GPT-4o Mini to replicate those responses.

Knowledge distillation is a known technique in the field of  classic machine learning; it involves copying the teacher model’s soft probability outputs and training the student to match them. 
But with LLMs, especially large proprietary models, you usually don’t have access to internal probabilities. 
However, we don't necessarilly need these. Modern LLM distillation is much simpler. The student model learns to imitate the final output of the teacher—no logits, no temperature scaling, no special access.  This is known as <a href="https://arxiv.org/abs/1606.07947">sequence-level distillation</a>.

---
**Remark: Sequence Level Distillation**

Sequence-Level Knowledge Distillation (Seq-KD) is a model compression technique where a smaller "student" model is trained to replicate the global output sequences of a larger "teacher" model, rather than matching individual token or classification probabilities.  Introduced by Kim and Rush in 2016, this method uses pseudo-labels generated by the teacher (typically via beam search) as training targets, allowing the student to learn the teacher's holistic distribution and decoding behavior. 

This approach enables significant model compression, with studies showing up to a 13× reduction in parameters for Neural Machine Translation (NMT) and 9.8× reduction for Speech Recognition, while maintaining competitive performance.  By training on the teacher's full sequence outputs, students often perform better under greedy decoding and exhibit reduced sensitivity to exposure bias, effectively transferring the teacher's ability to generate coherent, structured sequences. 

Recent advancements have expanded Seq-KD beyond simple text translation to include speech recognition, audio captioning, and continual learning scenarios.  Modern variations address limitations like memorization amplification and long-tail data generalization through techniques such as f-divergence minimization (e.g., f-DISTILL), curriculum learning, and multi-stage balanced distillation, ensuring robust knowledge transfer across diverse modalities and complex reasoning tasks.

---

Here is how we can use sequence-level distillation when working with LLMs. First you run a set of domain-specific prompts through the teacher model and capture its responses.
You then  fine-tune a smaller student model using those input–output pairs. The result is a student that knows how to generate the same output when given the same input in essence mimicking the teacher’s behavior. This approach is easy to implement and works well in practice. This is especially true when the teacher has already been fine-tuned for the task
Knowledge distillation addresses three of the biggest challenges in LLM production [1]:

- Cost: Distilled models are typically 5–20× smaller than their teachers, with lower memory and compute requirements.
- Latency: Smaller models respond faster—critical for live applications like chatbots or assistants.
- Flexibility: Distilled models can run in constrained environments like mobile devices, browsers, or edge servers.

Below are some best practices when excersing knowledge distillation [1]:

- Use a strong teacher. The student can only learn what the teacher demonstrates.
- Use diverse examples. Capture a variety of inputs that reflect real-world usage.
- Evaluate frequently. Check quality across tone, format, and correctness—not just accuracy.
- Retrain as needed. When your domain or user needs evolve, regenerate data and update the student.

Overall, knowledge distillation makes it possible to take a high-quality, fine-tuned LLM and make it practical for deployment. By training a smaller student to mimic the teacher’s outputs, you preserve quality while gaining speed, efficiency, and scalability. If you’re serious about bringing LLMs into production with a lower cost, distillation should be part of your workflow.


## Summary

Knowledge distillation is a technique for making large language models practical for production by training a smaller “student” model to imitate the behavior of a larger “teacher” model. While large models such as GPT-4 or LLaMA-2 65B can achieve excellent performance, they are often too expensive, memory-intensive, and slow for real-world deployment. Distillation addresses this by transferring the teacher’s knowledge into a lightweight model that is faster, cheaper, and easier to scale. Modern LLM distillation commonly uses sequence-level distillation, where the student learns from the teacher’s generated outputs rather than internal probabilities or logits. The process typically involves generating high-quality input–output examples from the teacher and then fine-tuning the student on those examples. Distilled models reduce inference cost and latency while remaining effective for domain-specific tasks, making them suitable for deployment on edge devices, mobile systems, and large-scale applications. The text also highlights best practices such as using strong teachers, diverse training examples, continuous evaluation, and periodic retraining to maintain performance as requirements evolve.
